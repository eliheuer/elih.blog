Libre Type as a Veblen Good

August 3, 2022

I have been experimenting with new business models for independent free/libre/open-source font development using NFTs. Enough people have been curious about what I'm doing that it seems like the right time to put some of my thinking into an essay. I'm not going to go into much detail about the thinking behind libre type, but anyone involved in the font business who wants to understand this stuff should read Dave Crossland's 2008 University of Reading MATD Dissertation and Richard Stallman's 1985 GNU Manifesto. I also won't go into detail explaining NFTs, but Type Designer and founder of the MFEK font editor project Fredrick Brenan has some good commentary on NFTs here.

In economic theory, goods are often classified based on their unique economic properties. Fonts, particularly for the Latin script, are commonly sold as snob goods, a good where the main attraction is related to its image of being expensive, exclusive, and a symbol of social status. These goods will have a restricted supply and only be available to people with high income. Snob goods have the property that as more people people buy (or pirate) them, the desirebility of that good decreases. For example, there was a time when the typeface Favorit was fresh and new. Being a standard neo-grotesque with a few distinctive characters, it's use was a way for publishers to signal taste and class to consumers in-the-know. But over time, as more people become familiar with the typeface, and more people buy pirate and use the typeface, newer, less rinsed neo-grotesque go on the market and become more attractive to snob goods consumers.

Nobody is forced to be a proprietary type designer. Most of us cannot manage to get any money for standing on the street and making faces. But we are not, as a result, condemned to spend our lives standing on the street making faces, and starving. We do something else.

I like libre fonts. I like being able to modify them, combine them (think Arabic + Latin), get them for free, use them in open source work, and avoid complicated licenses and CDNs.
