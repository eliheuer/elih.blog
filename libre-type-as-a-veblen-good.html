Libre Type as a Veblen Good

Eli Heuer (Draft 001)

Eli Heuer 2022, image license: CC0 ‚ÄúNo Rights Reserved‚Äù

I have been experimenting with new business models for independent libre (open source) font development recently. I had my first hint that these models might work when my project GTL Type Label minted all 100 editions of its first font NFT. Enough people have been curious about what I'm doing since then that it seems like the right time to put some of my thinking around libre type and NFTs into an essay.

GTL001 Alpha NFT floor price viewed from Rainbow App, image license: CC0 ‚ÄúNo Rights Reserved‚Äù

I'm not going into much detail here about the philosophy behind libre type and why I sometimes use the word "libre" instead of "open source", but I think that the cultural nature of typefaces, and the way they lend themselves to being forked to create new typefaces, makes fonts uniquely well suited to be licensed under a copyleft license like the OFL. Two documents are foundational to my thinking about licensing for font software and are recommended reading for anyone who wants to better understand my perspective:

1: Dave Crossland's 2008 University of Reading MATD Dissertation

2: Richard Stallman's 1985 GNU Manifesto.

I'm not a free software maximalist, and I'm well aware of the shortcomings of the ideas from the above documents in practice, as well as the problems with permissively licensed open-source software around value capture for developers and software freedom for users. Without my experiences and disappointments with the reality of free and open-source software development, I'm not sure if I ever would have become interested in Ethereum. What attracted me to this space was a culture interested in solving many of the misaligned incentives that I have noticed in web2 free and open-source software development. Specifically, projects like GitCoin, and concepts like Hyperstructures, NFTs, DAOs, quadratic funding, and retroactive public goods funding were all very interesting to me as I fell down the crypto rabbit hole.

The image was created with a Python script using DrawBot-Skia, a version of DrawBot that works on Linux. The script is included in the GTL001 Git repository if you want to use it or just take a look at how the image was made. I like this way of working beacuse I can automate rerendering all the images for a project using as Bash script, and anyone in the future that forks or contributes to this project will have human readable Python source files to work from, and won't need a Mac, Figma, or any Adobe products to use the source files. The auxiliary monospace text is set in Hasubi Mono, a Arabic/Latin monospace typeface I designed, which I'm currently preparing for a relese on Google Fonts.

What I'm trying to do with this approch is to create two seprate but linked goods with different economic properties, a public good, and a veblen good. Libre fonts are best understood in economic terms as public goods, here is the definition of public goods from Wikipedia (also a public good):

"In economics, a public good (also referred to as a social good or collective good) is a good that is both non-excludable and non-rivalrous. For such goods, users cannot be barred from accessing or using them for failing to pay for them. Also, use by one person neither prevents access of other people nor does it reduce availability to others. Therefore, the good can be used simultaneously by more than one person. This is in contrast to a common good, such as wild fish stocks in the ocean, which is non-excludable but rivalrous to a certain degree."

Image source: Twitter

And the definition of a veblen good, also from WikiPedia:

‚ÄúA Veblen good is a type of luxury good for which the demand increases as the price increases, in apparent (but not actual) contradiction of the law of demand, resulting in an upward-sloping demand curve. The higher prices of Veblen goods may make them desirable as a status symbol in the practices of conspicuous consumption and conspicuous leisure. A product may be a Veblen good because it is a positional good, something few others can own.‚Äù

Ether (ETH), the native token used by the Ethereum blockchain, is itself sometimes considered a veblen good. As I'm writing this, The Ethereum mainnet merge, which will reduce Ethereum's energy consumption by 99.95%, has been tentatively scheduled for around September 15th/16th 2022. The price of ETH earlier this year was around $1000 briefly, but with the merge date confirmed and the final testnet merge compleated, the price has grown to around $1900. I baught some ETH around 1000k (not nearly enough I now realize) but now at 1900k, my desier to buy ETH is much greater due to the increasing prices indication that other people want ETH. On the other hand, the software that runs Ethereum is a public good, this is the origin of the public/veblen good concept for me. Ethereum even uses a lot of copyleft licenses instead of permissive licenses for major parts of its ecosystem. Notably Go Ethereum AKA "geth", Solidity (the Smart Contract Programming Language), and the interface for Uniswap are licenses under the GPL-3.0 license.

If a font released under this public/veblen good model ever becomes a meme or gets a lot of use, the Alpha release specimen might be worth something as a collectible in the future. This would reward the people who originally minted the NFT, which could encourage people to follow and support type designers using this model and opsosed to designers using traditional digital font licensing schemes beacuse it creates a more positive sum game. If the font doesn't go anywhere after the alpha release, it's a relatively inexpensive NFT (GTL001 was 0.009ETH at mint) that is a public receipt (viewable from web3 apps like Rainbow or Zora) for funding independent open source software development. Ideally, and this is what I'm really excited about, font NFT could create better incentive for contributing to open source font projects, a NFT holder might made issues on the Git repo hoping the floor price will go up for example. I'm in the process of adding a sign-in-with-Ethereum button to the GTL website, this would allow me to provide utility like bonus content, discounts on print specimens and merch, and earlt access to other NFT mints to people signed in that are holding specific NFTs.

Image source: NFTs I own, viewed from Zora

The final paragraph of Dave Crossland's MATD Thesis almost seems to predict a positive-sum bussiness model like this emerging, and beacuse I worry most people in the font indstry have never actully finished reading it, I will quote him here:

"While today‚Äôs professionals use proprietary business models, the biggest problem facing new type designers is obscurity. Treating customers as friends rather than thieves and type design as a service instead of a product may prove an effective way for newcomers to enter the market while reinforcing a free society in the age of computer networks."

I realize buying an NFT for a font that can be downloaded for free from a Git host like GitHub or Radicle might still seem insane to some people. Especially people looking at web3 from the outside, but imagine if no one had thought of proprietary font licensing as we know it today and tried to introduce it in 2022. It might seem strange to pay for an infinitely copyable font file, and then not have the ability to resell the license on a secondary market or make changes to the font to suit your own needs. Maybe font NFTs and proprietary font licenses are equally absurd ideas, one just seems normal because it is familiar and established.

I think that the proprietary font model could also be improved with NFTs. I have never purchased a proprietary font before, but I have baught many Nintendo games on cartridges. The great thing about buying software this way is I can trade games with a friend, I can sell old games or trade them in for other games at GameStop, and sometime, if you hold onto old games long enough, they become colectors items that can be sold for lots of money!

I can imagine a future where software like Figma has a sign-in-with-Ethereum button and you need to hold a specific NFT to use a proprietary font. That would be a lot easier than manually storing and keeping track of license files and information. Then you could have a secondary market for font licenses, I might actully buy my first proprietary font if I thought I could flip it for a profit.

I remember going to many type design lectures and classes at Cooper Union in the early-to-middle 2010s and hearing lots of complaints about Google Fonts and the OFL. As someone who is now an investor in crypto but didn't know the first thing about investing back then, I now recognize the sentaments expressed back then as "FUD", fear uncertenty and doubt. Everyone does this, I'm as guily as anyone else. We are all aware of things happening in the world that could hurt us or help us with our careers or finances, and we conciously or uncociously promote the things that will help us and disparage the things that will hurt us when we speak. Today I hear a lot of similar FUD around NFTs in the font indusrty, and my my hope is just that more people will see past it and play around with new bussiness models using this technology. I'm excited to see what the libre type commons will look like by the end of this decade, in web3 everyone gets to be Dave Crossland for 15 minutes.
